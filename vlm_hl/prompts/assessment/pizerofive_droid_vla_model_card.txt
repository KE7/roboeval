The robot that is being controlled is a Franka Emika Panda 7-axis robot arm with a two-pronged gripper as its end effector, operating on a tabletop environment (specifically, an environment from the DROID robot demonstration dataset). The means of control for this robot arm is through the usage of a *Vision-Language-Action* Model, or VLA for short. VLAs are machine learning models based primarily on the Transformer neural network architecture that are trained to complete visuomotor tasks provided in natural language end-to-end. VLAs are generally constructed by fine-tuning a vision-language model (VLM, i.e. a large language model extended with vision capabilities) on a large-scale dataset that pairs visual observation and language instructions with robot trajectories. These models combine a vision-language encoder (typically a Vision-Language-Model or a vision transformer), which translates an image observation and a natural language description into a distribution within a latent space, with an action decoder that transforms this representation into continuous output actions, directly executable on the robot. VLAs are trained on large-scale datasets that contain teleoperated robot demonstrations. These datasets contain camera image inputs from a variety of angles and distances, and contain robot demonstrations from a number of settings, such as office tabletops, kitchens, research labs, and so on.

The particular VLA that is executing the robot in the current environment has been fine-tuned with a small number of robot demonstrations to adapt to the current environment setting and camera input. However, fine-tuning the VLA on particular tasks does not guarantee that those tasks will always be successful during the deployment of the fine-tuned VLA on this robot. Since the VLA only sees a camera image and the robot's current configuration as its input, it is prone to making errors in accordance with those limitations. For example, ambiguous language instructions, configurations that are out-of-distribution, or potentially confusing environment scenarios can cause the VLA to act differently from the task it was instructed to accomplish when deployed. The current VLA being deployed tends to pay attention to certain descriptors in the task instruction, such as color, shape, spatial orientation, or the names of objects, but is not guaranteed to perfectly attend to these descriptors.
